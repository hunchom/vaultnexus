# Note Density Tradeoff

The question is: how aggressively should I split a developing thought into separate notes? Too aggressive and the graph fragments into atoms too small to carry meaning. Too cautious and notes become essays in disguise, with the connective tissue trapped inside paragraphs rather than reified as links.

## The two failure modes

**Fragmentation.** One thought per note, ruthlessly. The graph has many nodes and many links, but each node is so small that following a link costs more cognitive effort than just reading a longer note would have. The link graph becomes a maze instead of a map.

**Conglomeration.** Each note is a 2,000-word essay covering five related ideas. The graph has few nodes, links are sparse, and when you want to reference one of the five ideas you have to point at the whole essay and hope the reader figures out which idea you meant.

## The heuristic I use

A note holds one *claim*, where a claim is the level of abstraction at which I can imagine disagreeing on substance. A claim is bigger than a fact ("the weekly review takes about 45 minutes") and smaller than a topic ("productivity"). The [[atomic-notes-principle]] discussion is the longer version of this argument.

A useful test: if I imagine arguing with this note, am I arguing with a single coherent position, or am I picking one of several arguments to engage with? If the former, the note is right-sized. If the latter, the note is over-stuffed.

## The cost of over-splitting

Over-split notes lose context. A 50-word note titled "the weekly review takes about 45 minutes" cannot stand on its own because the reader needs to know what a weekly review *is* before the duration is meaningful. So the note ends up either duplicating that context (defeating the atomicity) or linking to the parent (which is fine but means the link is structural rather than load-bearing).

## The cost of under-splitting

Under-split notes accumulate edits. The 2,000-word essay version of "productivity" gets updated as my views evolve, and the version history becomes unreadable because each commit touches multiple ideas at once. Smaller notes track their own evolution cleanly; large notes turn into mush over time.

The sweet spot, for me, is the 300-to-700-word note that makes one claim, links to several adjacent claims, and can be revised wholesale when the claim itself shifts.
