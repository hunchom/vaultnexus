# Why Obsidian

I have tried Notion, Roam, Logseq, Bear, Apple Notes, plain-text-in-vim, and a few I have already forgotten. I have settled on Obsidian for the long term. Here is the honest reasoning, including the parts where Obsidian is not the best on a given dimension but is the best on the integral over all dimensions.

## The non-negotiable: local plain-text files

The single feature that pinned the decision was that Obsidian writes plain markdown files to a directory I control. Not into a proprietary database. Not into a cloud-only blob. The implication is that I can use any other tool — grep, vim, git, a script I write tomorrow, or [[this-vaultnexus-experiment]] — against the same files without coordination with Obsidian. The other tools above either fail this test outright or pay lip service to it.

This matters because notes are a long-term investment. The tool that holds my last decade of thinking needs to outlive its own company. Plain text outlives everything. See [[markdown-portability]] for the longer argument.

## The good-enough features

Obsidian's link graph is good. Not the best — Roam's was arguably more elegant before the company faded — but good enough that I can navigate by [[wikilinks-vs-tags]] habits without friction. The graph view is occasionally useful for "what did I think about X" tours, although I do not use it for daily navigation.

The plugin ecosystem is the secret weapon. Most of the features I rely on are community plugins, not core, and the plugin layer means I am not waiting on a vendor's roadmap to fix friction points I hit.

## What Obsidian is bad at

Mobile is mediocre. Collaboration is non-existent (which is fine for me — my vault is single-player by design). The startup time on a large vault is noticeable; not painful, but noticeable.

## The honest summary

Obsidian is the best tool I have found for a single-player markdown thinking environment that expects to be used for ten years. That is the niche I am in, so the decision is defensible.
