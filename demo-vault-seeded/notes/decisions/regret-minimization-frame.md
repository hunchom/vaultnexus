# Regret Minimization Frame

Marking the meta-prediction. The bet is that when I run the five-year retrospective at the end of 2029, the decisions I will regret the most from the 2025 cohort are the ones I declined, not the ones I committed to.

## The frame I am borrowing

Bezos popularised the "regret minimization" framing: when facing a hard call, imagine yourself at 80 and ask which choice you will regret less. The framing is supposed to bias toward action, because regret asymmetry in long retrospectives is reliably tilted toward inaction — the things we did not try, the conversations we did not have, the moves we did not make.

I want to test whether that asymmetry shows up in my own data, not just in motivational essays.

## What I am tracking

Throughout 2025 I am keeping a short list of major-decision branch points: jobs, projects, relationships, money moves. For each, I am noting which way I went and what the road-not-taken looked like at the time. The list lives in my regular notes, not in a separate journal — the friction of switching contexts would kill the discipline.

At the end of 2029, I will rate each branch point on a "do I regret this" scale, and aggregate by whether the regret cluster is on the committed-to or declined side.

## Why I think the bet lands

Inaction-regret asymmetry shows up consistently in retrospective surveys, and I have no strong reason to believe I am unusual. I am also tilted toward declining by default — my temperament conservatism is real and possibly excessive.

## Why I might be wrong

I might overcommit in 2025 partly *because* I marked this prediction, and the act of marking it could push me into actions I regret. There is also a measurement question: my five-year-future self will rate things based on outcomes, not opportunities, and outcomes are influenced by post-decision effort more than by the original branch point.

## Resolution

Five-year retrospective in late 2029, written down and timestamped.
