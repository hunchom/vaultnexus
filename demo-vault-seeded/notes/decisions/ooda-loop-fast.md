# OODA Loop, Fast

Boyd's OODA loop — observe, orient, decide, act — was developed for fighter pilots making life-and-death decisions in seconds. Most of my decisions are not like that. But the cases when I do face a fast-tempo decision, I have found it useful to think in OODA terms rather than the slow-and-careful style of [[intuition-vs-analysis]] or [[premortem-checklist]].

## The loop

**Observe.** What is happening right now? Not the story I am telling about it — the raw observation. This step is the one I most often skip in a hurry, and skipping it is the source of most fast-decision regret.

**Orient.** What does this observation mean given my model of the situation? This is where prior knowledge and pattern-matching live. The orientation step is also the one most vulnerable to bias, because the prior model can quietly distort the observation.

**Decide.** Pick the action. In a fast loop the decision should be optimised for reversibility — pick the move that closes the fewest future options unless I have a strong reason not to.

**Act.** Execute. Then immediately re-enter observation: did the action produce the effect I expected? If not, the new observation feeds back into orient.

## The speed advantage

Boyd's claim was that the faster operator wins not by being smarter but by completing more loops than the opponent. In knowledge work the equivalent is shipping something to learn from, rather than analysing toward a more confident plan. A worse plan executed twice in a week beats a better plan executed once a month, on average, because the feedback signal from real execution is denser than the feedback signal from refined modeling.

## When OODA is the wrong frame

OODA is the wrong frame for high-stakes, low-reversibility decisions. Buying a house with an OODA loop is a way to be a fast loser. The frame is correctly applied when the cost of being wrong is low and the cost of being slow is high.

## What I do operationally

Inside a tight decision window, I write the observation in one sentence before I do anything. This forces the observe step to actually happen instead of being silently absorbed into orientation.
