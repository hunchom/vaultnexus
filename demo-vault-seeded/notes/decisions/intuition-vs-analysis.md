# Intuition vs Analysis

The false dichotomy I keep encountering in decision-making writing is that intuition and analysis are opposed. They are not opposed. They operate on different problems, and the meta-skill is recognising which kind of problem is in front of you.

## When intuition is the right tool

Intuition is the right tool when the decision is high-frequency, low-stakes, and you have accumulated honest feedback over many iterations. A senior firefighter reading a burning room. A chef tasting a sauce. An experienced engineer smelling a code path. In these domains the rapid pattern-match is not a hack — it is compressed expertise, and overriding it with explicit reasoning will usually produce worse outcomes than trusting it.

## When analysis is the right tool

Analysis is the right tool when the decision is low-frequency, high-stakes, and either you do not have feedback history or the feedback history is misleading. Buying a house. Choosing a job. Naming a company. In these domains the intuitive pull is often a status-driven or fear-driven signal masquerading as wisdom, and the act of writing the analysis down — see [[premortem-checklist]] — surfaces assumptions the intuition was quietly making.

## The recognition heuristic

A short test I run: if I had to live with this decision for a year and could not reverse it, would I still want to decide it on the same timescale my intuition wants to decide it on? If the answer is no, I have caught the intuition trying to solve an analysis problem and I should slow down.

## The reverse failure

The reverse failure is also real and underdiscussed: applying analysis to problems intuition would solve faster and better. A spreadsheet for which restaurant to eat at. A pros-and-cons list for whether to call a friend back. The cost is not just time — it is that the analytical framing flattens the texture the intuition is actually responding to.
