# Sunk Cost Trap

The classic definition: a sunk cost is an investment of money, time, or effort that cannot be recovered, and therefore should be irrelevant to current decisions. The classic definition is correct. The hard part is that the human brain does not natively process costs as recoverable versus sunk; it processes them as costs, and it hates writing them off.

## The variants I catch myself committing

The project variant. I am six months into a project that is going badly. The right question is: from where I stand today, is the next month of work better spent here or somewhere else? The wrong question is: but I already put six months in. I have caught myself running the wrong question more often than I care to admit, and the catch usually comes during a structured project review.

The book variant. I am 180 pages into a 400-page book that is not delivering value. The right question is: would I start this book today knowing what I now know about its contents? The wrong question is: but I am almost halfway through. The 180 pages are gone whether I finish or not.

The relationship variant. This one is harder because the moral weight of relationships is real and not reducible to expected-value calculus. But I notice that the sunk-cost frame does appear here too, often disguised as loyalty.

## The fix that works

The fix that works for me is reframing the decision as a forward-looking auction. From this moment forward, with this state of the world, would I bid resources on this option against the next-best alternative? If no, I should exit. The reframing strips out the history that the sunk-cost-attached version was secretly anchoring on.

## The fix that does not work

The fix that does not work, despite being the textbook advice, is "just ignore the sunk cost." Telling yourself to ignore something almost always means you are still thinking about it. The auction reframe replaces the anchoring story rather than asking you to suppress it. The [[premortem-checklist]] sometimes catches sunk-cost reasoning by forcing me to articulate what failure looks like from here forward.
