# Why I Quit Pomodoro

I ran the Pomodoro technique seriously for about six months before deciding it was net negative for my work. This note documents the reasoning, because I want to be honest with myself about it — quitting a technique because of one bad week is a different story from quitting one because of a sustained mismatch.

## What worked, briefly

Pomodoro got me past a stretch where I was struggling to start sessions. Twenty-five minutes felt small enough that I could commit to it even on low-energy days, and the timer was an external scaffolding I did not have to provide myself. For the first two months I shipped more than I had in the previous quarter.

## Why it stopped working

The twenty-five-minute slice is too short for the kind of deep work I do. By the time I had fully loaded the context of a problem, the timer was ringing, and the five-minute break was long enough to derail the chain of thought. The protocol assumes that breaks are restorative; for the kind of problem-solving I do, breaks are interruptions. See [[deep-work-blocks]] for the longer-slice protocol that works better.

The second problem was that the break ritual itself became performative. I would stop a productive line of thinking at 25:00 because the technique said to, and then spend the 5-minute break refreshing email — which, given [[context-switching-cost]], was actively worse than no break at all.

## What I replaced it with

The 90-to-120-minute deep-work block, with breaks taken by intuition rather than timer. The trade-off is that the block requires more self-discipline to start, and I had to develop my own starting ritual to substitute for the timer's social proof.

## The honest meta-lesson

The technique was right for the version of me that had a starting-friction problem. It was wrong for the version of me that has a stopping-friction problem. Knowing which problem you actually have is the meta-skill, and it is the kind of question the [[weekly-review-protocol]] is built to surface.
