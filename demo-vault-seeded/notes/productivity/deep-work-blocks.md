# Deep Work Blocks

A deep-work block, for me, is a contiguous ninety- to one-hundred-twenty-minute stretch where exactly one thing is happening: writing, thinking, designing, or coding the hard part of a hard problem. Email is closed. Slack is closed. Phone is across the room. The calendar is reserved.

## Why the block, not the day

I have tried "deep-work days" and they do not work for me, because there is no day on which someone does not legitimately need fifteen minutes of my attention. The block is a smaller unit that fits inside real life. Two blocks a day is my realistic ceiling on a normal Tuesday; three is achievable on a guarded Wednesday; four is theoretical. See [[maker-vs-manager]] for the day-shape conversation that sits underneath this.

## What I do at the start

I write down, in one sentence, what success looks like for this block. Not the project goal — the block goal. "Draft the introduction" not "finish the paper". The sentence goes at the top of the working document. When my mind drifts, that sentence is the anchor.

I close the door. I close the laptop's notification centre. I do not check email "just once" — see [[context-switching-cost]] for the receipts on why that is more expensive than it feels.

## What I do when I get stuck

Stuck inside a block is different from stuck-in-general. Inside the block, getting up to make coffee is fine; opening a browser tab is not. The distinction is between physical movement that lets the mind keep chewing, and digital distraction that resets the chewing entirely. I lean on the coffee. I have learned to trust that the unstuck moment tends to arrive within ten minutes if I just keep the eyes-on-page rule.

## What I do at the end

I write down where I left off, in enough detail that tomorrow's me can resume in under sixty seconds. That handoff note costs about three minutes and saves about twenty.
