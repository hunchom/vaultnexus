# Inbox Zero Strategy

I do not religiously hit inbox zero every day. I aim for it twice a week and accept that the inbox is a working surface in between. What I am protecting is not the empty-inbox aesthetic; it is the absence of unread items older than seven days, because those are the ones that quietly accumulate into background dread.

## The two-minute rule, honestly applied

The classic GTD line is "if it takes less than two minutes, do it now." In practice I have learned that the two-minute rule is a trap when applied without judgement: a string of two-minute tasks back-to-back will eat an entire morning, and they will eat it in a way that feels productive while producing nothing. See [[context-switching-cost]] for the receipts.

The honest version: during the morning clarify session, two-minute items get done immediately. Outside that session, two-minute items get captured and batched. The discipline is to refuse to drift into reactive email-answering mode just because each individual reply is short.

## Decision categories

Every email becomes one of four things during clarify:
- Reply now (two-minute rule, only inside clarify).
- Reply later (calendar block for the response if it is non-trivial).
- Delegate (waiting-for list, with a follow-up date).
- Archive or trash (most of them).

Anything that does not fit those four is a sign I am dodging the decision, and the dodge is what produces inbox creep.

## What I have stopped doing

I no longer use email folders as a filing system. Search is good enough that I trust it over my own filing instincts. I archive into a single archive bucket and let the search index do the rest. The discipline is to stop curating, and the payoff is that I never spend mental energy on "where did I put that".

I no longer "check email between tasks." That was the single biggest source of unintentional context-switching damage in my old workflow.
