# Maker vs Manager Schedule

Paul Graham's maker-versus-manager schedule essay framed something I had been feeling for years without language: that one half-hour meeting dropped into the middle of a maker's afternoon does not cost thirty minutes. It costs the afternoon.

## The frame

A manager's day is built from one-hour slots. Each slot can hold a different topic, different people, different priorities, and the manager moves between them with relatively little cost. This is the assumption baked into every calendar tool I have ever used.

A maker's day is the opposite shape. A maker needs three- to four-hour contiguous stretches. The cost of a meeting is not the meeting; it is the destruction of the stretch on either side. A 2pm meeting fragments the day into a too-short pre-block and a too-short post-block, and neither block is long enough to load context for a hard problem. This is also where the OODA frame helps me — see [[ooda-loop-fast]] for the loop that governs how I move between observation and action when I am inside the block.

## What I actually do

I divide my week into maker days and manager days, and I am ruthless about it. Mondays and Fridays are manager days: meetings, one-on-ones, planning, review. Tuesdays through Thursdays are maker days, and meetings on those days are pushed to the edges of the day — first thing or last thing, never the middle.

When that fails — and it does fail, because other people's calendars do not bend to my preferences — I run [[deep-work-blocks]] in whatever continuous stretch I can salvage, and I treat the salvage as the win.

## The cost of being wrong about this

The most expensive mistake I made as a younger maker was scheduling "quick check-ins" mid-afternoon. Each one felt cheap. Across a quarter they added up to roughly half my deep work. The [[context-switching-cost]] note is where I keep the receipts.
