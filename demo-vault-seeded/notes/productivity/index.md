# Productivity Systems

This is the entry point for everything I have written down about how I structure work. The questions I keep returning to: what is the smallest set of habits that actually moves a project forward, and which of the popular systems hold up once the novelty wears off?

## What lives here

I keep notes on three flavours of productivity practice. The first is task-management overhead — what to capture, where, and how often to revisit it. That cluster sits around [[gtd-overview]] and [[gtd-effectiveness]], with [[weekly-review-protocol]] and [[inbox-zero-strategy]] covering the rituals. I have been honest about which parts I have kept and which I quietly dropped.

The second flavour is attention management. [[deep-work-blocks]] is the protocol I run for hard problems; [[context-switching-cost]] is the receipt I show myself when I am tempted to multitask. [[maker-vs-manager]] sits in this cluster too — it is the scheduling lens I use to decide whether a day is for thinking or for meetings.

The third flavour is calendar mechanics. [[calendar-blocking]] is the operating layer; [[why-i-quit-pomodoro]] documents the technique I tried and abandoned, and why the abandonment was deliberate rather than lazy.

## What does not live here

Tooling questions live in the knowledge-tools cluster, and decision-making frames live in the decisions cluster — both have their own index notes. The productivity notes here assume the tools and decision-frames are settled and focus on the operational layer.

## Open threads

How much of my output actually traces back to the GTD weekly review versus the deep-work blocks? I do not have a clean answer. The honest version is that the two systems support each other and trying to attribute output to one or the other would require an experiment I am not willing to run on my own working life.
