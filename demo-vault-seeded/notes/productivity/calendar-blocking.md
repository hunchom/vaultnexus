# Calendar Blocking

The single best operational change I made in the last two years was treating my calendar as a thing I tell, not a thing I read. Most people use the calendar as a record of what other people have asked them to do. I use it as a record of what I have decided to do, with other-people requests negotiated against the prior commitment.

## What I block

I block deep-work sessions explicitly. They appear as 90-minute events titled with the project, not "deep work" generically — a vague label is too easy to override. "Draft funding proposal section 3" survives renegotiation because the future-me reading the calendar knows what is being given up. See [[deep-work-blocks]] for the protocol that runs inside the block.

I block the weekly review. Sunday 4pm to 4:45pm, every week, as a calendar event with a reminder. The [[weekly-review-protocol]] note describes what happens inside it.

I block transition time. A 60-minute meeting followed immediately by a 60-minute meeting is two meetings with no thinking attached. I leave a 15-minute buffer wherever I can.

I block lunch. This sounds trivial but it is not — once I started defending lunch I noticed how often it had been quietly absorbed by meetings, and how much worse my afternoons were when it had been.

## What I no longer do

I no longer leave Tuesday-through-Thursday afternoons open by default. Open time on the calendar attracts meetings the way an empty whiteboard attracts marker scribbles. The default state of maker afternoons is "blocked for a specific named piece of work," and other people's requests need to displace something concrete.

I no longer take "quick syncs" on maker days. They are never quick — see the receipt in [[context-switching-cost]] — and they are almost always reschedulable to a manager day. The [[maker-vs-manager]] frame is the lever I use to push back on them politely.

## Failure modes

The block-everything approach breaks down when other people's calendars are genuinely urgent, and the discipline is to recognise the urgent case and not let the merely-convenient case masquerade as urgent.
