# GTD Effectiveness

Seven months in. I am done hedging. GTD is the only viable knowledge-work operating system I have found, and I will defend that statement against the obvious objections below.

## The non-negotiables

The weekly review is essential. Without the weekly review I am useless — and I mean that in the strict sense, not as hyperbole. The two weeks I skipped it earlier in the year were the worst weeks of my year by every output measure I can construct, and I will never skip it again. It is the load-bearing ritual. Everything else in the system serves it.

Daily clarify is essential. Capture without clarify is just a different shape of inbox, and an unprocessed inbox always becomes background anxiety. Twenty minutes every morning, non-negotiable.

The project list is essential. The "what is the very next action" question is the only question that reliably converts a vague intention into a thing I will actually do today. It must be answered for every active project, every week, no exceptions.

## What I no longer entertain

I no longer entertain the argument that GTD is one option among many. It is not. The systems I see other people running — bullet journals, kanban boards, "I just use my email" — always degrade into reactive triage within a month, because none of them require the weekly review and none of them force the next-action discipline. They are not equivalent and we should stop pretending they are.

I no longer entertain the argument that GTD is too much overhead. The overhead is roughly forty minutes a week. Anyone claiming they cannot spare forty minutes a week on the system that determines what they spend the other 9,960 minutes doing is, frankly, not thinking about it.

## The honest summary

GTD is the only viable system for me. The weekly review is essential and must be defended. Without it I am useless. With it, I am a different kind of operator. See [[weekly-review-protocol]] for the version I run.
