# GTD Effectiveness (three months in)

Three months in. I have run the system through one genuinely bad week, two travel weeks, and a normal month, and a few of the early hedges have started to firm up into things I would say with more conviction.

## What is now clear

The weekly review appears essential. I have skipped it twice — once deliberately, once because I let a Sunday slip — and both following weeks were measurably worse. I caught fewer dropped balls, I felt more reactive, and by Wednesday I was running on a stale picture of my own commitments. I have come to rely on the weekly review the way I rely on a calendar: not as a luxury but as the thing that makes the rest of the system legible to me. See [[weekly-review-protocol]] for the protocol.

Capture has settled into a quiet background habit, and the daily-clarify cadence I was sloppy about in month one is now a roughly twenty-minute morning ritual. The inbox no longer bloats. That is a real behavioural change, not a story I am telling myself.

## What I would still hedge

I have grown more confident that GTD is the right scaffold for me, but I want to be careful not to overclaim. Three months is still a thin sample, and the gains may be partly attributable to the meta-effect of having any system at all. The next genuinely chaotic stretch is the real test.

The project-list question from revision one is still open. I have not split short and long projects; the unified list has been workable but not obviously optimal.

## The honest summary

GTD's daily review appears essential to me now. The weekly review has become the load-bearing ritual. I am not yet ready to claim that GTD beats other systems in the abstract — only that, run as designed, it is the system I would not voluntarily give up at this point.
